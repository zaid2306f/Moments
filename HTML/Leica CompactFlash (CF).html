Speed: CompactFlash cards came in different speed classes, which determine how quickly data can be read from or written to the card. These speed classes are often indicated in terms of "x" (e.g., 133x, 600x) or in megabytes per second (MB/s). 4. Durability: CompactFlash cards are known for their robust design. They are built to withstand more physical stress and harsh environmental conditions, making them suitable for professional and outdoor photography. 5. Usage: CF cards were widely used in digital cameras, including some high-end models produced by Leica. They could also be used in various other devices, such as audio recorders, industrial equipment, and older PDAs. 6. Compatibility: To use a CompactFlash card in a device, it must have a CompactFlash slot. Some cameras and devices offer compatibility with CF cards through an adapter or an additional slot. 7. Data Transfer: CompactFlash cards typically used the IDE/ATA interface for data transfer, which differs from the more modern SD cards that use the SPI interface. It's worth noting that CompactFlash cards have become less common in recent years, with many devices now using SD cards or microSD cards due to their smaller size and compatibility with a wider range of electronics. However, CF cards are still in use among certain professionals who require their specific advantages, like increased durability and faster data transfer rates.
